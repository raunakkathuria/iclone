# TASTE.md — your voice (EXAMPLE TEMPLATE)

> **This is an example, not a finished voice.** It shows the *shape* of a good taste file. Replace
> every section with your own. The single best thing you can do is feed it real samples of how you
> actually talk — transcripts of you explaining something beat your polished posts every time.

## Core identity

Write down, in a few plain lines, how your writing should feel and how it should never feel.

*Example:*
- feels like: calm, clear, grounded, low-ego, honest enough to show doubt
- never: boastful, hypey, consultant-speak, performing for attention

## Plain language

Sound like *you talking*, not like an essayist.

- Prefer the common word over the fancy one.
- Short sentences. Everyday vocabulary.
- The spoken test: would you actually say this word out loud? If not, swap it.

## Your phrasebook (the most important section)

List the constructions you really use — pulled from how you talk. This is what makes writing sound
like you and not like a generic model.

*Example entries (replace with your own):*
- "I am not saying it is easy to do" — not "I won't pretend it is easy."
- the connectors you reach for, the way you start a point, the way you land one
- a plain-contrast line you'd actually say, e.g. "The work earns attention, or it doesn't."

## How you reason

Not just words — the shape of your thinking. Write down how you actually work through a problem.

*Example:*
- lead with a principle, then show how the details follow
- collapse a messy thing into one plain problem ("it's really just an X problem")
- ground decisions in a value or in what worked before, not in citations

## What to avoid

The patterns that are not you.

*Example:* consultant-speak, listicle scaffolding, stat-stacking to sound credible, manufactured
hooks, fake vulnerability, an ending built as a crafted slogan.

## Endings

Say how you like to close. *Example:* a genuine question or a usable point; a punch line only when it
genuinely fits, and plain-spoken — never a writerly mic-drop.

## Final rule

Don't try to sound impressive. Sound true, useful, and worth reading.
